---
title: "How permissions work"
description: "Roles, exceptions, and the review queue — the whole model in five minutes."
---

Everything in MCP Gateway reduces to one sentence:

> **A person may do an action on a kind of record only if you said so.**

The console gives you three instruments to say so efficiently.

## 1. The permission grid

Every permission is a cell in a grid: rows are kinds of records (contacts,
deals, tickets, email drafts…), columns are the four things anyone can do to
them — **read, create, update, delete**. Grids are shown per connected
system, so HubSpot decisions and Gmail decisions never blur together.

*[screenshot: permission grid for one integration]*

A cell that isn't ticked doesn't merely "not work" — the ability is
**invisible** to that person's AI. You can't misuse what you can't see.

## 2. Roles — decide once, apply to many

A role is a saved set of grid choices with a name: *Read-only*, *Sales rep*,
*Support agent*. You assign a role to each person; changing the role later
changes everyone who holds it, at once.

**Exceptions** handle the real world: one person can differ from their role
by individual cells — extra ability granted, or one removed. Exceptions are
marked with a dot in the grid, so deviations are always visible at a glance
rather than buried.

<Note>
Rule of thumb: if you're making the same exception for the third person,
it wants to be a role.
</Note>

## 3. The review queue — nothing new sneaks in

Connected systems evolve: an update to your CRM's integration can introduce
brand-new abilities (a new "merge records" action, say). In most software,
new abilities just… appear.

Here they land in the **Review queue**, switched off, with a plain
description and a flag when they deserve extra caution (destructive actions,
sending on someone's behalf). They stay off until you explicitly approve
them. The same applies to abilities that *changed shape* since you approved
them — they lose their approval and return to the queue.

*[screenshot: review queue]*

## Everything is on the record

Three kinds of events share one audit trail, in order:

- every action the AI performed, under the name of the person it acted for
- every action that was **denied**, and why
- every rule change an admin made — who granted what to whom, when

That combination answers the question audits actually ask: *not just what
happened, but who made it possible.*
